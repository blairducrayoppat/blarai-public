#940 Your decision

Should BlarAI pursue custom, accented voices — and if so, when?

The concept: voice cloning

BlarAI currently speaks through Kokoro, a small local text-to-speech model with 54 stock voices — all of them American or British English. Thursday's audition confirmed none of them is the voice you want, and none can be. A different local model, XTTS v2, works another way: give it a clean one-minute recording of any voice and it speaks new text in that voice — this is voice cloning. The test workbench we built proves it runs on this machine and sounds more natural than Kokoro.

Two things Thursday taught us: clone quality is dominated by the reference recording (your phone clip produced a robotic clone — the recording was the problem, not the method), and there is no ready-made pack of the accents you want. Each accent — Scottish, Irish, Australian, Turkish, Ukrainian — means sourcing a clean, properly licensed voice sample individually. Scottish and Irish exist in a research archive (VCTK, free with attribution); the rest come from Mozilla's Common Voice collection (public domain), each a separate fetch-and-try.

The governance frame — two separate decisions, only one asked today

Exploring voices in the workbench changes nothing inside BlarAI. Putting a cloning engine into BlarAI is a capability change — a heavier model, new software dependencies, and voice licensing obligations — and would come back to you later as its own go-live ceremony, security-reviewed like everything else. The guardrail you set Thursday already binds all of it: only freely licensed or consented voices, never a clone of a real person without consent. Today's question is only about the research direction.

Your options

A — Park it.Recommended

Keep the workbench (about a gigabyte on disk, zero upkeep), do nothing now, revisit when the voice program comes back around. Nothing is lost — the findings, the working setup, and the sourcing plan are all recorded on the ticket. My reasoning: today's pace fix (#853, shipping separately) removes the one complaint you could actually hear, and your own insight from Thursday — that "character" comes mostly from personality (word choice, attitude) rather than voice timbre — points at a cheaper, higher-leverage lever first.

B — Pursue the accents now.

Per-accent sample sourcing and clone trials as background work — a few sessions spread over coming weeks, ending in an audition for you. Adoption into BlarAI would still be a separate, later decision.

C — Drop the direction.

Delete the workbench, keep the written findings. Frees the disk; restarting later means rebuilding the setup (about an evening's work).

Also on the table, but not asked today: persona design — the warm-witty and steady-heroic personality sketches from Thursday. If voice character matters to you, that is the lever I would pull before any accent work. Say the word and it becomes its own ticket.

How to answer

One line does it: "Park", "Pursue — start with ⟨accent⟩", or "Drop". If you'd like the persona-design ticket opened, add "and open the persona ticket."

#939 Confirm

The assistant ignores your "always answer in English" rule — fix deferred until the next model

Thursday's live test confirmed the defect is real: with the rule stored and active, a French question still gets a French answer. The investigation cleared the machinery — saving, editing, and injecting the rule into every conversation all verified working. What fails is the model itself: mirroring the language you write in is one of a small language model's strongest instincts, and a written instruction loses to it. Rewording the rule more strictly changed nothing, which proves wording isn't the lever.

Why deferring is the disciplined move

Rule-following of this kind lives in the model, not the plumbing — and the resident model is likely to be replaced (the Qwen3.6 direction, screen 4). Training or prompt-engineering obedience into a model on its way out is work thrown away. Instead, a permanent test case now rides in the evaluation suite: a French question paired with your English rule, marked as a known failure. The day a new model or fix closes the gap, that test flips green on its own — the re-evaluation is built into the system rather than left to memory. This is the same pattern the project uses everywhere: encode the promise as a test, then let the test keep the promise.

Transparency note: during Thursday's testing your stored preference was edited to the stricter wording "Always respond in English, even if I write to you in another language." That edit is live in your preference store; the original wording is preserved in the audit history.

How to answer

"Confirmed" keeps the deferral. If the day-to-day annoyance is worse than it looked, say so — there is an interim guard we could build sooner (a check that detects a wrong-language reply and regenerates it — a deterministic wrapper around the model, no retraining), at the cost of pulling effort forward.

#930 · #923 · #769 Not decidable yet

One model to replace three? Genuinely blocked — here's the honest state and the watch plan

The concept: consolidation onto a Mixture-of-Experts model

Today BlarAI's brain is Qwen3-14B for text plus a separate vision model, swapped in and out of memory as needed. The candidate to replace both is Qwen3.6-35B-A3B — a Mixture-of-Experts model: 35 billion parameters organized into specialist groups, of which only about 3 billion are consulted for any given word. Think of a firm with 35 specialists where each question is routed to the three relevant people — big-firm expertise at small-team speed. One resident model would handle chat and vision with no swapping, at the cost of more memory held at rest (~19 GB versus the 14B's ~8).

What the evidence says so far — favorable, but partial

  • Your own side-by-side judgment: the 35B gave better vision answers than the dedicated vision model — and ran faster per turn on this laptop.
  • Speed is confirmed healthy with the official build (~35 tokens/second — the earlier alarming number traced to a bad community conversion, not the model).
  • The official build handles images — confirmed.
  • On the standard 40-question answer-quality exam it passed 22 — but about 12 of the 18 failures carry one bug's fingerprints rather than bad answers (see below). The underlying content was frequently right.

The blocker — and why it's a governance problem, not a cosmetic one

Models like this "think out loud" before answering. Normally the thinking is wrapped in markers so BlarAI strips it before you see the reply. On this model, the runtime ignores the thinking off-switch and omits the markers — private reasoning leaks into replies, and, more seriously, BlarAI's Policy Agent (the gate that adjudicates every risky action) relies on that off-switch to get clean, classifiable output. A control surface the trust spine depends on doesn't work on this model today. A fix exists upstream — pull request #4139 to Intel's OpenVINO GenAI runtime, where we've posted supporting test data — but it has not been merged.

Why no decision today

This is a capability change, a quality change, and an architecture amendment in one — and every quality number we hold was measured through a known defect. Deciding now would mean judging the model by its symptoms. The disciplined sequence: the fix lands upstream → the exam re-runs clean → the genuine failures (a handful of prompt-injection and grounding misses) are isolated from the bug's artifacts → you get a complete decision package. Two further items ride in that package: parity checks for the Policy Agent's classification duties, and re-establishing the speed technique (speculative decoding) the current 14B enjoys and the newcomer would initially lose.

The watch plan

Ticket #923 checks the upstream fix on 2026-07-30. When it merges: clean re-run, isolation of the real failures, then the decision comes to you properly. Nothing is stalled that could be moving — the waiting is the correct state.

How to answer

Nothing today. If you want the watch checked more often than the 30th, say so.
